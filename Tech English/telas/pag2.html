Chapter: The Vision of Business in the Digital Age

How ubiquitous connectivity and the web lifestyle are reshaping organizational structures and value chains.

1. The Vision of Ubiquitous Digital Change

Summary: In the near future, high-speed internet and digital devices will be everywhere. All information and equipment will become digital, and users will carry portable data notebooks.

Future technological changes, predicted to be widespread within a decade, will fundamentally alter business. This includes:

Ubiquitous Computing and Connectivity: Computers and high-speed internet will be common in both the workplace and homes.

Digital Everything: Communication (email), information flow (bills, bookings), and equipment (phones, cameras) will all become digital.

Portable Data: Versatile digital notebooks will allow users to carry all personal and professional data.

2. The Digital Nervous System and Required Shifts

Summary: Companies must adopt a "digital nervous system." Interactions become self-service, humans focus on high-value advice, and executives must personally use digital tools.

Preparation for this change requires implementing a "digital nervous system" to handle three key shifts:

Digital Self-Service: Most interactions (B2C, B2B) will become digital and automated.

High-Value Customer Service: Human involvement will shift to providing high-value, personalized advice.

Internal Digitalization: Companies must adopt digital processes internally to remain adaptable. Executives are urged to personally use digital tools to understand this new world.

3. The Rule of Value for Middlemen

Summary: The internet connects buyers and sellers directly. Middlemen must either add significant personalized value or switch to low-cost automation to survive "disintermediation."

The Internet creates an efficient market where buyers and sellers connect directly, reducing the need for simple intermediaries.

Disintermediation: Simple middlemen (those who only transfer goods or information) are rapidly losing value.

The New Mandate: Intermediaries must add value (e.g., highly personalized services) or become high-volume, low-cost providers using self-service technology. Companies like Merrill Lynch exemplify this by investing in technology to empower their core assets (consultants) to provide higher-value advice.

4. Embracing the Web Lifestyle

Summary: The "Web lifestyle" changes how people get news and shop. Educated customers demand better service, shifting the sales team's role to that of a consultant.

Businesses must use the internet for creative customer interaction, focusing on online experience and service.

Explosive Growth: Online sales are growing exponentially.

Educated Customers: Companies find that informed customers are better customers, shifting the sales team's role to a consultant.

Societal Shift: The "Web lifestyle" is enabling people to turn to the internet for news, entertainment, finance, and community, broadening horizons and strengthening cultural connections.

5. Speed and Flexible Boundaries

Summary: Digital information flow changes organizational structures. Companies should outsource non-core functions to scale efficiently and focus on their core skills.

Digital information flow changes organizational structure and processes.

Focus on Core Skills: Companies should outsource non-core functions to scale efficiently and focus on their core competencies.
